The Importance of Electricity

Answer: Electricity.

From power tools for carpenters to control systems for plumbers and HVAC technicians, a solid understanding of electricity is essential for safety and success in any skilled trade.

The Building Block: The Atom

Everything in the universe—from the copper in a wire to the air we breathe—is made up of atoms.

"An atom is the smallest particle of an element that still has the properties of that element."

To understand electricity, we must first understand the structure of the atom.

[Image of atom structure model]

Inside the Atom

An atom has two main parts: the Nucleus and the orbiting Electrons.

The Nucleus (Center)

  • Protons: Have a POSITIVE (+) electrical charge.
  • Neutrons: Have NO charge (they are neutral).

Orbiting Particles

  • Electrons: Have a NEGATIVE (-) electrical charge.
  • They are very small and orbit the nucleus at high speed.

Atomic Neutrality

Key Point

In a normal atom, the number of electrons (-) is equal to the number of protons (+).

This makes the atom electrically neutral.

The Most Important Part: Electron Shells

  • Electrons don't just orbit randomly; they stay in specific paths called shells or energy levels.
  • Think of it like planets orbiting the sun in our solar system.
  • Each shell can only hold a certain number of electrons.
  • The shell furthest from the nucleus is the most important one for electricity.
[Image of electron shells diagram]

The Valence Shell - Key to Electricity

The outermost shell of an atom is called the VALENCE SHELL.

The electrons in this shell are called valence electrons.

The flow of electricity is the flow of electrons.

How easily an electron can leave its valence shell determines if a material will be a good conductor of electricity or not.

If a valence electron can be easily "knocked out" of its shell, it can move freely and contribute to electric current.

Session 1.2: Charge, Force, and Materials

What is Electric Charge?

Charge is a fundamental physical property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field.

It's the property that we assigned to Protons (+) and Electrons (-).

The Coulomb (C)

We measure electric charge in a unit called the Coulomb.

Analogy: Just like we measure distance in 'metres' or mass in 'kilograms'... we measure charge in 'Coulombs'.

1 Coulomb = the charge of approx. 6.24 quintillion electrons! (6.24 x 1018)

Think of it like this: instead of counting individual grains of sand, we count bags of sand. The Coulomb is like a "bag" that contains a massive number of electron charges.

1 Ampere (A) = 1 Coulomb of charge flowing past a point in 1 second.

The Law of Charges: Coulomb's Law

The fundamental rule of how charges interact is simple and universal:

Opposite Charges ATTRACT

A positive charge and a negative charge will pull toward each other.

Like Charges REPEL

Two positive charges will push away from each other.

Two negative charges will also push away from each other.

This law of attraction and repulsion is the force that makes electrons move.

[Image of Attract and Repel Diagram]

Materials: Conductors

Definition: Materials that allow electric charge (electrons) to flow through them easily.

Atomic Structure

They have 1 to 3 valence electrons that are loosely held by the nucleus. These "free electrons" can be easily pushed from one atom to the next.

Examples

  • Best: Silver, Copper, Gold, Aluminum
  • Good: Brass, Steel, most other metals.

Copper is the most common conductor used in electrical wiring because it offers a great balance of conductivity and cost.

Materials: Insulators

Definition: Materials that resist or prevent the flow of electric charge.

Atomic Structure

They have 5 to 8 valence electrons. Their valence shell is full or nearly full. Electrons are held very tightly to the nucleus and are not free to move.

Examples

Rubber
Plastic
Glass
Porcelain
Dry Wood
Air

Insulators are essential for safety, used to cover wires and protect us from electric shock.

Materials: Semiconductors

Definition: Materials that are neither good conductors nor good insulators.

Atomic Structure

They have exactly 4 valence electrons. Their properties can be precisely controlled by adding impurities (a process called "doping").

Examples

  • Silicon (Si)
  • Germanium (Ge)

Semiconductors are the foundation of all modern electronics: diodes, transistors, computer chips, solar panels, etc.

Summary Table

Property Conductors Insulators Semiconductors
Valence Electrons 1-3 (Few) 5-8 (Many) 4 (Exactly)
Electron Mobility Very High (Free) Very Low (Bound) Can be controlled
Resistance to Flow Very Low Very High Moderate

Module 1 Recap

  • All matter is made of atoms.
  • Atoms contain protons (+), neutrons, and electrons (-).
  • The valence shell is the key to electrical properties.
  • Conductors have few valence electrons and let current flow easily.
  • Insulators have many valence electrons and block current flow.
  • Semiconductors have 4 valence electrons and are used to control flow.
  • The law of charges states that opposites attract and likes repel.

Module 2: Electrical Quantities

Session 2.1: EMF and Electric Current

The "Big Three" of Electricity

To understand any electrical circuit, you must know these three key quantities:

  • Voltage (V): The electrical pressure or "push."
  • Current (I): The rate of electrical flow.
  • Resistance (R): The opposition to flow.

We will use a simple water pipe analogy to understand how they relate to each other.

Electromotive Force (EMF) / Voltage

Definition: EMF, commonly called Voltage, is the potential difference or pressure that causes electrons to move in a circuit.

It's the "push" that forces electrons away from a negative point and towards a positive one.

Unit of Measurement: The Volt (V).

Without voltage, there is no push, and therefore no current can flow.

Water Analogy: Voltage is Pressure

High Voltage: Like a full water tower. Lots of pressure, potential to push a large amount of water.

Low Voltage: Like a nearly empty water tower. Little pressure, only pushes a small amount of water.

[Image of Water Tower Voltage Analogy]

Where Does Voltage Come From?

Voltage is created by converting other forms of energy into electrical energy.

1. Chemical Source: Cells & Batteries

How it works: A chemical reaction inside the cell creates a buildup of electrons at the negative terminal (-) and a shortage at the positive terminal (+), resulting in a voltage.

  • Primary Cells: Non-rechargeable (e.g., standard AA, AAA batteries).
  • Secondary Cells: Rechargeable (e.g., car batteries, phone batteries).

2. Magnetic Source: Generators

How it works: Based on the principle of electromagnetic induction.

When a wire is moved through a magnetic field, a voltage is "induced" in the wire.

This is how almost all the world's electricity is generated.

3. Other Sources

Thermal (Thermocouples): Heating a junction of two different metals creates a small voltage. Used for temperature sensors.

Light (Photovoltaic Cells): Light striking a semiconductor material (like silicon) knocks electrons free, creating a voltage. This is how solar panels work.

Electric Current

Definition: Electric Current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

It's the measure of how many electrons are moving past a point in a certain amount of time.

Unit: The Ampere (A), often called "Amps".

Symbol: I (from French 'intensité').

Current is what does the work (lights a lamp, turns a motor).

Water Analogy: Current is Flow Rate

High Current: Like a wide pipe with lots of water flowing through it (many litres per second).

Low Current: Like a narrow pipe with only a trickle of water flowing through it.

Relationship: Current & Charge

Remember: Charge (Q) is in Coulombs, Current (I) is in Amps.

1 Ampere is defined as 1 Coulomb of charge flowing past a point in 1 second.

Q = I x t
Q = Charge (Coulombs)
I = Current (Amps)
t = Time (Seconds)

Calculation Practice

Question 1

A current of 5 Amps flows through a lamp for 2 minutes. What quantity of electricity (charge) is transferred?

Step 1: 2 mins x 60 = 120 sec
Step 2: Q = I x t
Step 3: Q = 5 A x 120 s
Answer: Q = 600 C

Question 2

If 540 Coulombs of charge passes through a point in 2 minutes, what is the current?

Step 1: I = Q / t
Step 2: 2 x 60 = 120 s
Step 3: I = 540 C / 120 s
Answer: I = 4.5 A

Module 2: Electrical Quantities

Session 2.2: Resistance & Current Flow

Conventional vs. Electron Flow

The Big Question: Which way does electricity actually flow?

This is confusing, but simple once you know the history.

There are two "correct" answers:

  • Electron Flow: What is physically happening.
  • Conventional Flow: The standard used for calculations.

You MUST understand both.

Electron Flow Theory (Physical Reality)

  • Electrons are negatively charged.
  • The negative terminal has a surplus of electrons.
  • The positive terminal has a shortage.
  • Opposites attract, likes repel. Electrons are repelled from negative and attracted to positive.
Electrons flow from NEGATIVE (-) to POSITIVE (+)
[Image of Electron Flow Diagram]

Conventional Flow Theory (Industry Standard)

  • Early scientists (like Ben Franklin) assumed electricity was a positive fluid flowing from high (+) to low (-) potential.
  • This convention was established before the electron was discovered.
  • It was too late to change all the books!
We assume current flows from POSITIVE (+) to NEGATIVE (-)
[Image of Conventional Flow Diagram]

Seeing Both Flows

Rule of Thumb:

Thinking Physics?

Think Electron Flow. (What is actually happening).

Reading Schematics / Calculating?

You MUST use Conventional Flow.

Resistance

Definition: Opposition to the flow of electric current.

A measure of how difficult it is for electrons to pass through.

Resistance converts electrical energy into heat.

Unit: The Ohm.

Symbol: Omega (Ω).

Water Analogy: Obstruction

Low Resistance: Wide, clear pipe. Water flows easily.

High Resistance: Narrow, rusty pipe. Difficult to push water through.

Four Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance of a conductor depends on four key factors:

1. Length
2. Cross-Sectional Area
3. Material Type
4. Temperature

Let's look at each one.

Factor 1: Length

The longer a conductor, the higher its resistance.

Analogy: It's harder to push water through a very long hose than a short one due to friction.

Reason: Electrons must travel past more atoms; each collision causes opposition.

Resistance is directly proportional to length.
(Double length = Double resistance)

Factor 2: Cross-Sectional Area (CSA)

The thicker a conductor, the lower its resistance.

Analogy: A wide fire hose has less resistance than a narrow garden hose.

Reason: A thicker wire provides more paths for electrons to flow.

Resistance is inversely proportional to area.
(Double area = Halve resistance)

Factor 3: Material Type (Resistivity)

Every material has its own natural opposition called resistivity.

  • Conductors have very low resistivity.
  • Insulators have very high resistivity.

Copper

1.68 x 10-8 Ω·m (Very Low)

Glass

1010 to 1014 Ω·m (Extremely High)

Factor 4: Temperature

For most conductors, as temperature increases, resistance increases.

Reason: Heating causes atoms to vibrate more rapidly.

This makes it harder for electrons to find a path, leading to more collisions.

Note: Semiconductors typically decrease resistance as they get hotter.

Module 2 Recap

  • Voltage (V): Pressure that pushes current. Measured in Volts.
  • Current (I): Rate of flow. Measured in Amps.
  • Resistance (R): Opposition to flow. Measured in Ohms (Ω).
  • Conventional Flow: (+ to -) is used for analysis.
  • Resistance depends on Length, Thickness, Material, and Temperature.

Module 3: Ohm's Law & Circuit Analysis

Session 3.1: The Simple Circuit & Ohm's Law

The Electric Circuit

A circuit is a closed loop that allows current to flow.

Four Essential Parts:

  • Source: Provides voltage (Battery).
  • Path: Conductors (Wires).
  • Load: Uses electricity (Lamp).
  • Control: Opens/Closes (Switch).

A Simple Circuit Diagram

Here are the four parts working together:

When the Control (Switch) is closed, the loop is complete. Current flows from Source -> Path -> Load -> Source.

[Image of Simple Circuit Diagram]

Circuit Conditions

Open Circuit

The path is broken (open switch, broken wire, blown fuse).

Result: No Current Flows.

Infinite Resistance.

Short Circuit

Unintended low-resistance path bypassing the load.

Result: Dangerously High Current.

Fire Hazard. Wires melt.

Fuses protect against this.

Module 3: Session 3.2

Ohm's Law

"The current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it and inversely proportional to the resistance, provided all physical conditions remain constant."

Increase Voltage (Push) = Current Increases
Increase Resistance (Opposition) = Current Decreases

The Formula

V = I x R
  • V = Voltage (Volts)
  • I = Current (Amps)
  • R = Resistance (Ohms)

The Triangle Method:

Cover V -> I x R

Cover I -> V / R

Cover R -> V / I

[Image of Ohms Law Triangle]

Calculation Practice

Example 1

A simple circuit has a 230Ω resistor connected to a power source. If a current of 5 Amps is flowing, what is the source voltage?

Known: R = 230Ω, I = 5A

Unknown: V

Formula: V = I x R

Calculation: V = 5A x 230Ω

Answer: V = 1150 Volts

Working with Large & Small Numbers

We use engineering prefixes to make numbers easier to write.

IMPORTANT: You must convert all values to their base units (Volts, Amps, Ohms) before doing calculations!

Calculation with Prefixes

Example 2

A circuit has a resistance of 2 kΩ and a current of 50 mA is flowing. What is the voltage?

Step 1: Convert
R = 2 kΩ = 2000 Ω
I = 50 mA = 0.05 A

Step 2: Formula V = I x R

V = 0.05 A x 2000 Ω

Answer: V = 100 Volts

Lab: Verifying Ohm's Law

Goal: Prove that V = I x R.

  1. Measure Resistance: With power OFF, measure your resistor's ohms. Record it.
  2. Build Circuit: Set up the power supply and resistor.
  3. Set Voltage: Turn on and set to 5 Volts.
  4. Measure Voltage: Connect voltmeter in parallel. Record.
  5. Measure Current: Power OFF. Connect ammeter in series. Power ON. Record.
  6. Calculate: R = V / I. Compare to your Step 1 measurement.

Effects of Current

Heating Effect

Electrons colliding with atoms generate heat.

Used in toasters, fuses.

Magnetic Effect

Current generates a magnetic field.

Used in motors, generators.

Chemical Effect

Current causes chemical reactions.

Used in batteries, electroplating.

Electrical Safety

Your #1 Priority

Electricity is invisible and gives no warning. Respect it, always.

"There are old electricians and there are bold electricians, but there are no old, bold electricians."

The Human Body & Electricity

Why is electricity dangerous? Because the human body is a conductor.

Our nervous system works using tiny electrical signals. External current can override these signals with disastrous results.

[Image of Human Nervous System Diagram]

Effects of Current (AC)

1 mA
Can just be felt (perception level).
5 mA
Shock is painful.
10-20 mA
Muscle contractions. "Let-go" threshold.
50-100 mA
Ventricular Fibrillation. Often fatal.

It takes very little current to kill.

Main Electrical Hazards

Electric Shock

Current passing through the body. Path across heart is critical.

Burns

Caused by current heating tissue or contact with hot components.

Arc Flash

Explosive energy release. Fatal burns, pressure waves, shrapnel.

Fire

Overloaded circuits or short circuits igniting materials.

Basic Safety Rules

  • ALWAYS assume a circuit is live until proved dead.
  • Use a known, working voltage tester.
  • Lockout/Tagout (LOTO): Isolate and lock power. Saves lives.
  • Use insulated tools and PPE.
  • Never work live unless necessary and trained.
  • Keep work area clean and dry.

Key Takeaways

Respect all voltages.
A tiny current can kill.
Test Before You Touch.
Lockout / Tagout saves lives.
