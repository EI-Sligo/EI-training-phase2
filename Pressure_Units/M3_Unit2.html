What is a Manometer?

A device that measures pressure by balancing a column of liquid against the pressure being measured.

The Principle

The pressure at the same horizontal level in a continuous fluid is equal. We calculate pressure difference using:

ΔP = ρgh

ρ = density | g = gravity | h = height difference

[Image of U-Tube Manometer]

Types of Liquid Manometers

U-Tube

Simple "U" shape. Measures difference in height between two limbs. Basic and reliable.

Single Limb (Well)

One wide reservoir and one narrow tube. Easier to read as only one level changes significantly.

Inclined Limb

Tilted tube stretches the scale. High Precision for very low pressures (Drafts).

Digital Manometers

Uses an electronic transducer (Piezo-resistive/Capacitive) instead of liquid.

Pros

  • No hazardous liquids (Mercury).
  • Portable & Durable.
  • Easy to read display.

Cons

  • Requires battery power.
  • Needs regular calibration.
[Image of Digital Manometer]

Hazards

Toxic Liquids

Mercury is highly toxic if inhaled or absorbed. requires spill protocols. Water/Oil can cause slips or contamination.

Over-Range

Applying too much pressure can blow the liquid out of the tube, causing spills, injury, or process contamination.

Glass Breakage

Traditional manometers are fragile. Broken glass + Mercury = Major Hazard.

Accurate Reading

1. Levelling & Zeroing

Must be perfectly level (use spirit level) and zeroed before use.

2. The Meniscus

  • Concave (Water): Read the BOTTOM of the curve.
  • Convex (Mercury): Read the TOP of the curve.

3. Parallax Error

Eye must be level with the meniscus to avoid reading errors.
