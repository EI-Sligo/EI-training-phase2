Tools & Safety

Use instruments to measure lengths, diameters, and bores of given components.

Piping

Key Measuring Instruments

Steel Rule

For lengths. Accuracy: 0.5mm.

Vernier Caliper

For OD, ID, and Depth. Accuracy: 0.02mm.

Micrometer

For precision OD. Accuracy: 0.01mm.

Combination Square

For 90° and 45° angles and depth.

Dividers

For transferring dimensions.

Protractor

For measuring angles.

Care of Instruments

Best Practices

  • Cleanliness: Wipe away oil, dirt, and sweat after use.
  • Storage: Keep in a dry environment (case) to prevent rust.
  • Protection: Protect sharp points and measuring edges. Don't stack them.
  • Lubrication: Light oiling of moving parts (e.g., micrometer spindle).

The Micrometer

  • Ratchet Stop: Always use the ratchet to apply consistent pressure. Never overtighten using the thimble.
  • Zero Check: Before measuring, close the anvil and spindle to check it reads 0.00.
  • Accuracy: Typically 0.01mm.
Reading: Sleeve (Main Scale) + Thimble (0.01 Scale).

Marking Out

Datum Line/Point

A fixed reference edge or point from which all measurements are taken to ensure accuracy.

Marking Blue

A dye applied to metal to make scribed lines visible. Safety: Use sparingly, avoid skin contact.

Tools

  • Scriber: Fine steel point for marking lines.
  • Centre Punch: Indents metal to seat a drill bit.
  • Protractor: For angular measurement.

The Hacksaw

  • Frame: Adjustable for different blade lengths.
  • Blade: Teeth must point FORWARD (away from handle). Cutting action is on the push stroke.
  • Stance: Feet apart, balanced. Use full length of the blade.

Filing

Types & Action

  • Single Cut: For finishing/sharpening.
  • Double Cut: For rapid material removal.
  • Cross Filing: Diagonal strokes for removal.
  • Draw Filing: Sideways motion for fine finish.

Care

  • Pinning: Clogged teeth scratch work.
  • File Card: Wire brush used to clean files.
  • Handle: Never use a file without a handle (Safety).

Drilling Machines

Work Holding

Workpiece security is critical. Use a machine vice or clamps. Never hold work by hand.

Bit Holding

  • Jacobs Chuck: For straight shank drills. Always remove the key!
  • Morse Taper: For larger tapered shank drills. Use a Drift to remove.

Drill Speeds & Feeds

Small Drill = High Speed. Large Drill = Slow Speed.

Formula: RPM = (1000 x V) / (π x D)

0 RPM

Drilling Process

  • Centering: Always Centre Punch the mark to prevent drill wander.
  • Pilot Hole: For large holes, drill a small pilot hole first to reduce pressure.
  • Coolant: Use soluble oil/coolant to reduce heat and preserve the bit.
  • Swarf Disposal: Use a brush to clear swarf. NEVER use hands.

Tapping (Internal Threads)

  • Tap Wrench: Used to hold the tap.
  • 3 Tap Set:
    1. Taper: Starts the thread.
    2. Intermediate: Deepens it.
    3. Plug: Finishes to bottom of blind hole.
  • Lubrication: Trefolex or Oil is essential.

Tapping Drill Calculation

To tap a hole, the drilled hole must be smaller than the thread size.

Drill Size = Thread Size - Pitch

Example 1 (M6):
Pitch = 1.0mm
6mm - 1.0mm = 5.0mm Drill
Example 2 (M10):
Pitch = 1.5mm
10mm - 1.5mm = 8.5mm Drill

Workshop Safety Factors

Personal

  • Eyes: Safety glasses must be worn (Swarf hazard).
  • Hands: Beware of sharp edges. No gloves at rotating machines.
  • Stance: Correct body balance when sawing/filing.

Workpiece

  • Security: Workpiece must be clamped tight. Loose work can spin and cause injury.
  • Swarf: Metal shavings are razor sharp. Use a brush.

Piping Materials

Copper

Easy to bend. Used for low pressure air and water. Limitations: Can react with certain chemicals (Acetylene/Ammonia). Soft.

Stainless Steel

High tensile strength. Used for high pressure and corrosive fluids. Properties: Work-hardens (gets harder as you bend it).

Pipe Specifications

Sizes (OD)

Common Instrumentation sizes (Imperial):

  • 1/4" (6.35mm)
  • 3/8" (9.52mm)
  • 1/2" (12.7mm)

Wall Thickness

Determines the pressure rating. Thicker wall = Higher Pressure.

Pressure Rating

Stainless steel handles much higher pressure than copper. Check manufacturer tables.

Cutting Pipe

Pipe Cutters (Wheel)

Clean, square cut. Disadvantage: Creates a large internal burr that restricts flow.

Hacksaw

Rough cut. Requires filing.

CRITICAL: You MUST Ream (Deburr) the pipe after cutting. Burrs cause turbulence and damage fitting seals.

Bending Pipe

Hand Bender

Lever type benders used for 1/4", 3/8", 1/2".

  • Match pipe size to former groove.
  • Setback: Allowance for the bend gain.
  • Angles: 45°, 90°, Offsets.

Precautions

  • Do not flatten/kink the tube.
  • Stainless Steel Work Hardens: Bend it right the first time. Re-bending makes it brittle/hard.
  • Watch out for "flying" handles (Springback).

Fittings

Common names: Stud (Connector), Coupling (Union), Elbow, Tee, Cross.

Compression (Single Ferrule/Olive)

Uses a brass olive. Common in plumbing/low pressure copper.

Twin Ferrule

Industry Standard (Swagelok/Parker) for high pressure/instrumentation.

  • Front Ferrule: Seals.
  • Back Ferrule: Grips.

Twin Ferrule Assembly

  1. Cut tube square and REAM.
  2. Insert into fitting until it bottoms out.
  3. Tighten nut Finger Tight.
  4. Mark the nut (6 o'clock position).
  5. Tighten 1 and 1/4 turns (to 9 o'clock).

Danger: Never mix ferrule brands (e.g. Swagelok nut on Parker body). Bad joints cause high-pressure leaks/projectile hazards.

Inspection & Testing

Gap Gauge

Manufacturer's Distance Gauge. Checks if the fitting is tightened sufficiently (Go/No-Go test).

Thread Sealing

For threaded pipe joints (NPT/BSP): Use PTFE Tape or thread sealing compound. Tape goes clockwise.

Pressure Test

Pressurise system (air/water) to 1.5x working pressure. Check for leaks (Snoop/Soap water).
