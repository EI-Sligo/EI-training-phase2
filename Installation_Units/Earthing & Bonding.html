What is Earthing?

Earthing is the connection of the exposed conductive parts of an installation to the main earthing terminal of that installation.

Purpose:

  • Safety: To protect people and livestock from electric shock.
  • Protection: To limit the potential of current-carrying conductors with respect to the general mass of earth.
  • Operation: To provide a path for fault currents to flow, operating protective devices (fuses/MCBs). [Image of earthing system diagram]

Conductive Parts

Exposed Conductive Part

A conductive part of equipment which can be touched and which is not normally live, but which may become live under fault conditions.

  • Metal casing of a washing machine.
  • Metal conduit or trunking.
  • Motor housing.

Extraneous Conductive Part

A conductive part liable to introduce a potential, generally earth potential, and not forming part of the electrical installation.

  • Gas & Water pipes.
  • Structural steelwork.
  • Central heating radiators.
  • Stainless Steel Sink.

Main Equipotential Bonding

Connecting incoming metallic services to the Main Earthing Terminal (MET) to create an equipotential zone.

Requirements

  • Services: Gas, Water, Oil, Structural Steel.
  • Location: As close as possible to entry point (within 600mm).
  • Size: Minimum 10mm² (Domestic).
  • Connection: Must be continuous (unbroken).

Supplementary Bonding

Additional bonding used in locations of increased shock risk (e.g., Bathrooms).

Bathroom Rules

Connects all extraneous conductive parts and exposed conductive parts within the zones.

  • Hot & Cold water pipes.
  • Central heating pipes/radiators.
  • Bath/Shower waste (if metal).
  • Circuit protective conductors.

Earthing Conductor

Definition

The conductor connecting the Main Earthing Terminal (MET) of the installation to the Earth Electrode or other means of earthing.

Labelling

The connection to the earth electrode must be permanently labelled:

SAFETY ELECTRICAL CONNECTION - DO NOT REMOVE

Protection

Must be protected against mechanical damage and corrosion. Typically Green/Yellow insulation.

Earth Electrodes

A conductor or group of conductors in intimate contact with, and providing an electrical connection to, earth.

Rod Electrode: Copper-clad steel rod driven vertically into the ground. Most common.
Plate Electrode: Copper or Galvanized Iron plate buried deep in the ground.
Strip/Tape Electrode: Copper tape buried in a trench.

Soil Resistivity

The resistance of the earth electrode depends heavily on the soil conditions.

Moisture

Wet soil = Lower Resistance (Good).

Soil Type

Clay/Loam = Good.
Sand/Rock = Poor.

Temperature

Frozen ground increases resistance significantly.

Circuit Protective Conductor (CPC)

A protective conductor connecting exposed conductive parts of equipment to the main earthing terminal.

Sizing (Adiabatic Equation)

S = √(I²t) / k

  • S: CSA of conductor (mm²)
  • I: Fault current (Amps)
  • t: Operating time of device (seconds)
  • k: Factor for conductor material/insulation

Conduit as Protective Conductor

Steel Conduit

Allowed. Steel conduit can be used as the CPC provided electrical continuity is maintained.

Joints must be tight. Running couplers must have locknuts.

PVC Conduit

Never. PVC is an insulator. A separate CPC cable must be drawn into the conduit.

Flexible Conduit

Sometimes. The spiral metal armor alone is usually not sufficient. A separate CPC is required unless the flexible conduit is specifically designed and certified as a protective conductor.

TN-C-S System

Also known as PME (Protective Multiple Earthing).

  • Supply: The Neutral and Earth are combined in the supply network (PEN conductor).
  • Installation: They are separated at the consumer's intake (Main Earthing Terminal).
  • Advantage: Low earth loop impedance (Ze), reliable fuse operation.
  • Risk: Broken supply neutral can make installation metalwork live.

TT System

Common in rural areas (overhead lines).

  • Supply: No earth provided by supplier.
  • Installation: Consumer must provide their own earth electrode (Rod/Plate).
  • Impedance: Earth loop impedance (Ze) is usually high.
  • Protection: RCDs are essential for earth fault protection as fuses/MCBs may not operate quickly enough.

Testing Earth Electrode Resistance

3-Point Fall of Potential Method

  1. Disconnect electrode from installation (OFF load).
  2. Place Current Spike (C) and Potential Spike (P) in line with electrode (E).
  3. Pass current between E and C.
  4. Measure voltage between E and P.
  5. Meter calculates Resistance (R = V/I).

Earth Fault Loop Impedance (Zs)

Formula

Zs = Ze + (R1 + R2)

  • Zs: Total Loop Impedance.
  • Ze: External Impedance (Supplier's side).
  • R1: Resistance of Phase Conductor.
  • R2: Resistance of Protective Conductor (CPC).

Important: Zs must be low enough to trigger the MCB/Fuse within 0.4s.
