Conducting Materials

  • Copper: Very good conductor. Soft, malleable, easy to joint.
    The most common conductor used in domestic/house wiring.
  • Aluminium: Good conductor, light weight. Used for large power cables. Not for domestic circuits.
  • Silver: Best conductor but expensive. Used for switch contacts.
  • Tungsten: High melting point. Used for lamp filaments.

Safety: Dissimilar Metals

WARNING: Copper and Aluminium conductors should not be directly connected together.

Reason: They have different resistance and thermal expansion rates, leading to corrosion (Galvanic Action) and loose joints.

Insulators

  • PVC, Rubber, Porcelain.

Cable Construction

A standard cable consists of 3 parts:

  1. Conductor: Carries current (Copper).
  2. Insulation: Basic protection against shock (PVC).
  3. Sheath: The outer grey layer.
    Function: Mechanical Protection.
Definition: The size of a conductor is measured by its
Cross-Sectional Area (CSA) in mm².

Insulating Materials

PVC

Poly-Vinyl Chloride

Most common insulation. Tough, moisture resistant.
Temp range: 0°C to 70°C.

Rubber / Flex

Used for flexible cords where movement is required (irons, kettles). Rubber is flexible but ages/cracks over time.

ETCI Rule

The minimum size flexible cord allowed for fixed applications in a house (e.g. a pendant drop) is 0.75 mm².

Terminating Techniques

The Perfect Termination

  • Insulation: Must go right up to the metal of the terminal (No bare copper showing).
  • Doubling Back: Solid conductors should be doubled back to fill the terminal and prevent the screw cutting through the wire.
  • Tightness: Must be mechanically and electrically sound.
  • Carrying Capacity: Must carry load current without an increase in temperature.

Dangers of Loose Connections

The Chain Reaction:

  1. A loose connection reduces the contact area.
  2. This creates a High Resistance joint.
  3. Current flowing through resistance creates Heat (I²R).
  4. Heat causes insulation to melt/burn (Fire Hazard).

Hand Tools

Pliers & Snips

Pliers for twisting/gripping. Side cutters (snips) for cutting cables and trimming insulation.

Wire Strippers

Used to remove insulation without nicking the conductor. Nicked conductors break easily.

Knives

Electrician's knife used for stripping the outer sheath of large cables. Always cut away from yourself.

Fixing Devices

Woodscrews

Pointed Tip. Coarse thread. Used for fixing into timber or into wall plugs (red/brown plugs) for mounting backing boxes.

Machine Thread Screws

Blunt/Flat End. Fine thread. Used for fixing switch and socket faces to their metal back boxes. (Typical Size: M3.5)

SWA Cable Bending Radius

SWA = Steel Wire Armour. Used for underground/industrial mains.

The Rule: 8 x D

The internal bending radius for SWA is typically 8 times the overall diameter.

Example Calculation:

Cable Diameter: 20mm

Calculation: 20 x 8 = 160

Minimum Radius = 160mm

Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance of a conductor depends on (Formula R = ρ x L / A):

  • Length (L): Longer cable = Higher Resistance.
  • Cross-Sectional Area (A): Thicker cable = Lower Resistance.
  • Material (ρ): Copper has lower resistance than Aluminium.
  • Temperature: Heat increases resistance.
Important: Resistance does NOT depend on the Insulation type.

Voltage Drop

What is it?

When current flows through a cable, some voltage is "lost" along the way due to the resistance of the copper.

How to Reduce it?

To reduce voltage drop, we must reduce the resistance.

Solution: Increase the Cross-Sectional Area (CSA) of the conductor. (Use a thicker cable).

Current Ratings & Temperature

The current carrying capacity of a cable depends on how hot it gets.

The standard tables in the ETCI Rules for current rating and voltage drop are based on a specific ambient temperature.

Regulating Bodies

ETCI

Electro-Technical Council of Ireland. Produces the National Rules for Electrical Installations (The Rules). Purpose: Safety.

RECI / Safe Electric

Register of Electrical Contractors of Ireland. Regulates contractors.

IEC

International Electro-technical Commission. Global body for electrical standards.

CENELEC

European Committee for Electro-technical Standardisation. Harmonises standards across Europe.

Safety Criticals

Stripping

Never nick the conductor. It reduces CSA and causes heating.

Termination

Tighten screws sufficiently. Double back conductors.

Selection

Use correct cable size for the load. Use correct sheath for the environment.

Standard Cable Sizes

Typical applications for domestic PVC/PVC:

  • 1.5mm²: Lighting Circuits.
  • 2.5mm²: Socket Circuits (Radial/Ring).
  • 6.0mm²: Showers / Cookers.
  • 10.0mm²: Electric Showers (High Power).
  • 16.0mm²: Main Tails (Meter to Consumer Unit).

Module Summary

  • Termination: Tight, clean, no bare copper outside terminal.
  • Safety: Loose connections cause fire.
  • Rules: 0.75mm min flex. 8xD SWA bend radius.
