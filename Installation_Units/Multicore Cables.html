Cable Types

NYM-J

PVC sheathed, insulated cable used for industrial/domestic surface wiring. Similar to "Hi-Tuff". Not armoured.

SWA (Steel Wire Armoured)

Heavy duty. Galvanised steel wires provide mechanical protection and earth continuity.

Field Instrument Cable

Twisted pairs (often screened) for carrying low voltage signals (4-20mA) and preventing interference.

SWA Construction

Essential for underground or hazardous area installation.

  • 1 Conductor: Plain Stranded Copper.
  • 2 Insulation: XLPE (Cross-linked Polyethylene) or PVC.
  • 3 Bedding: PVC layer cushioning the insulation.
  • 4 Armour: Steel Wires (Mechanical & Earth).
  • 5 Sheath: Black PVC (UV Resistant).
Quiz Tip: SWA is one of the few cables permitted to be buried directly in the ground (Min depth 600mm).

SWA Termination

Cable Glands

  • BW Gland (Indoor): Clamps armour only. Two-part design.
  • CW Gland (Outdoor): Clamps armour AND seals outer sheath (Waterproof). Three-part design.

The "Banjo" (Earth Tag)

Must be used to ensure earth continuity between the armour and the enclosure. The "fly-lead" connects the tag to the earth terminal.

Cable Tray Systems

Perforated Tray

Standard duty. Holes allow ventilation and cable tying.

Ladder Tray

Heavy duty. Used for large power cables over long spans.

Basket (Mesh)

Lightweight. Easy to shape/bend on site. Good for data/instrumentation.

Segregation: Band I (Data) and Band II (Mains) cables must be segregated (separated) on trays to prevent interference.

Resistance & Resistivity

The resistance (R) of a conductor depends on:

  • Length (l): Directly proportional (∝). Longer = Higher R.
  • Area (a): Inversely proportional (R ∝ 1/a). Thicker = Lower R.
  • Material (ρ): Resistivity (Rho). Copper is better than Aluminium.
R = (ρ × l) / a

Where ρ = Resistivity (Ωm), l = Length (m), a = Area (m²).

Practical Example

Copper vs Aluminium:
Copper and Aluminium should NOT be directly connected because they corrode when in contact (Galvanic corrosion).

Temperature Coefficient

Effect of Heat

The resistance of a pure metal conductor increases as its temperature rises.

This is defined by the Temperature Coefficient of Resistance (α).

Rₜ = R₀ (1 + αt)
Rt = Final Resistance
R0 = Initial Resistance
α = Temp Coeff
t = Temp Rise

Note: Cable rating tables in ETCI rules are typically based on an ambient temperature of 30°C.

Voltage Drop

Every cable has resistance. Current flowing through resistance causes a voltage drop (V = I x R).

Factors Affecting Volt Drop

  • Length: Longer cable = More drop.
  • Load Current: Higher current = More drop.
  • Conductor CSA: Thinner cable = More drop.
  • Ambient Temp: Heat increases resistance.
  • Grouping: Bundling cables retains heat.
Solution: To reduce voltage drop, you must Increase the CSA of the cable.

Power Loss (I²R)

Current flowing through a cable generates heat. This is wasted power.

P = I² × R

Example:
If Current (I) doubles, the Power Loss (P) quadruples (because 2² = 4).

Eddy Currents: Another form of loss/heat caused by AC magnetic fields in ferrous (steel) conduit/trunking. Prevent by putting all phases in same conduit.

Fibre Optic Cables

Uses light signals instead of electricity. Immune to Electromagnetic Interference (EMI).

Core: Glass or plastic centre where light travels.
Cladding: Reflective layer keeping light in the core.
Safety: Never look into the end of a fibre cable (Laser hazard). Shards of glass fibre are dangerous to skin/eyes.
